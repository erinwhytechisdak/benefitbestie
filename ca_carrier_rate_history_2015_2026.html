How This Data Exists
AB 2118 — California Assembly Bill 2118 (2020)

Before 2020, there was no standardized requirement for health plans to publicly report aggregate premium rate data in a consistent, comparable format. AB 2118 changed that. Signed into law in 2020 and effective for measurement year 2021, it requires every DMHC-regulated health plan to annually report premiums, cost sharing, enrollment, and trend factors for individual and small group products — and mandates that the DMHC post it publicly and present it at annual meetings.

That's why clean, structured small group data only goes back to 2021 in the DMHC's compiled reports. Everything prior relies on rate review filings and public meeting presentations — which existed but weren't assembled into consistent carrier-level comparison tables.

★  TL;DR — Why Do All the Lines Look the Same Before 2024?

Think of the shaded region like a class GPA. The DMHC publishes one single blended number for the entire California small group market each year — it tells you how the class performed on average, but not how each individual student did. Before 2024, that blended market-wide average is the only figure available in any compiled public table — so all five carriers are shown using the same number as a stand-in.

That doesn't mean Kaiser and Aetna filed identical rates every year — they almost certainly didn't. It just means their individual report cards weren't published in summary form. From 2024 onward, the DMHC started releasing carrier-by-carrier tables in their public meeting documents, which is where the lines finally diverge and show real differences.

Want the actual pre-2024 carrier breakdown? Visit wpso.dmhc.ca.gov/dashboard/Finances.aspx, select each carrier, set market type to Small Group, and note the annual figures shown. Share those numbers and this chart updates to reflect real carrier-level history for the full 10 years.
