How This Data Exists
SB 546 (2015) · SB 17 (2017) · AB 731 (2019)

SB 546 requires every DMHC-regulated large group health plan (101+ employees) to annually submit aggregate rate information and weighted average rate increases. First filings were due October 2016 for measurement year 2016 — which is why confirmed large group data starts there, five years before comparable small group data existed under AB 2118. Renewal notices must compare the plan's rate change to both CalPERS and Covered California — making a built-in 3-way benchmark a legal requirement at every renewal.

SB 17 added a mandatory prescription drug cost overlay starting 2018 — requiring health plans to report Rx spending as a share of premium, the top 25 drugs by cost/frequency/increase, and PBM relationships. AB 731 (2019) layered on a formal rate review process for large group, effective July 2020.

★  TL;DR — Statewide vs Regional Plans
🌎 Statewide Plans

Operate across multiple regions statewide. In large group, the big names are Kaiser Permanente, Anthem Blue Cross, Blue Shield of CA, Health Net, Aetna, UnitedHealthcare, and Cigna. Kaiser alone represents 66% of all large group enrollment in California — so when Kaiser's rate goes up, it moves the statewide average significantly. Think of statewide plans as the national and near-national carriers that follow employer groups across geographies.

📍 Regional Plans

Operate primarily within specific geographic areas. In San Diego (Rating Area 19), the relevant ones are Sharp Health Plan and Scripps Health Plan. Others statewide include Western Health Advantage (Sacramento), Sutter Health Plus (NorCal), Contra Costa Health Plan, and Valley Health Plan (Santa Clara). They tend to have tighter provider networks but deeper local relationships — and as the data shows, they've consistently filed slightly lower rate increases than statewide carriers.

Why does the gap exist? Regional plans aren't carrying the full cost structure of a national carrier — no national network negotiation overhead, no cross-state risk pooling complexity. Their provider contracts are often tighter and more predictable locally. That said, they come with tradeoffs: employees can't use them if they live or travel outside the plan's service area, which matters for companies with distributed teams. For a San Diego-headquartered employer with most employees local, Sharp or Scripps can be a compelling cost alternative to a statewide carrier — especially in years where the statewide spread is 1–2 points or more.

What the Rx % line tells you: Out of every dollar your group pays in premium, Rx's slice has grown from 11.6¢ in 2018 to 14.5¢ in 2024. That's a 25% relative increase in pharmacy's share while medical's share actually compressed — partly because Rx is growing faster, and partly because manufacturer rebates have grown (hitting $1.2B in 2023) which slightly offsets gross drug costs in the net figure. What the Rx PMPM bars tell you: The actual dollar amount your plan is spending on pharmacy per member per month — going from roughly $61 in 2018 to $91.84 in 2024. That's a 50% increase in real dollars in 6 years. For context: if you have 200 employees, that's roughly $220,000 more per year in pharmacy spend than in 2018, just from trend. GLP-1s, specialty biologics, and oncology drugs are the dominant drivers. This is the strongest argument for PBM strategy, formulary management, and point solution conversations at renewal.

⚡  The Kaiser Question — What Does the Data Actually Say?
"Kaiser is always cheaper." Is the lore true?

This belief is deeply embedded in the California benefits world — and for years, it shaped renewal conversations, plan design strategy, and carrier negotiations. But when you pull the actual CDI SB 546 rate filings Kaiser files every year, the picture is more complicated than the lore suggests.

The short answer: historically, Kaiser ran above market — not below. From 2018 through 2021, Kaiser's self-filed large group rate increases exceeded the overall statewide market average every single year. The "Kaiser is cheaper" narrative appears to be more about premium level (what you pay per member per month) than rate trend (how fast that number is growing). Those are two very different things.

The Key Distinction

Premium Level vs Rate Trend — Two Very Different Things

Understanding why "Kaiser is cheaper" can be true and misleading at the same time

✓ Where the Lore Holds

Kaiser's base premium PMPM is generally lower than commercial carriers — averaging $572/mo in large group in 2023 vs $597–$692 for Anthem, Blue Shield, Health Net. Kaiser owns its hospitals, employs its physicians, and runs its own pharmacies, which creates genuine cost efficiencies at the point of care. For an employer doing a cost-per-member comparison at a single point in time, Kaiser often is cheaper.

⚠ Where the Lore Misleads

Kaiser's annual rate increases have historically tracked at or above market — meaning the cost advantage doesn't compound in your favor over time. From 2018–2021, Kaiser filed above-market increases every year. An employer who chose Kaiser in 2015 because it was "cheaper" has been riding those above-market trend increases for nearly a decade. The starting advantage erodes.

The 2024 flip is the most important data point. After two years (2022–2023) where Kaiser actually ran below market for the first time in the confirmed dataset — a pattern that could have suggested the lore was finally becoming true — Kaiser reversed course in 2024 with a +12.7% filing, 2 points above the market average. Kaiser itself cited its dual role as insurer and provider as the reason it "feels the effects of inflationary pressure on its cost structure faster than competitors." That's Kaiser acknowledging in writing that their integrated model creates cost exposure, not just cost savings.
